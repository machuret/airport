Who Can Be Sued for an Accident at {{airport_name}}

Accidents at {{airport_name}} involve multiple parties — and determining who is actually liable is one of the most important steps in any airport injury case. The airport, the airlines, and dozens of contractors all share different zones of responsibility.

In most cases, more than one party is liable. A slip and fall near a Starbucks at {{airport_name}} may involve the cleaning contractor, HMSHost, and the airport authority — all at once. We identify all of them.

Federal law adds another layer: TSA injuries require the Federal Tort Claims Act, and injuries on international flights may be governed by the Montreal Convention — both with strict deadlines different from standard personal injury claims.

Who we pursue at {{airport_name}}

{{airport_operator}} (Airport Authority)

Responsible for common areas, maintenance, security standards, and the overall facility at {{airport_name}}. Often a public entity requiring Notice of Claim.

Airlines Operating at {{iata_code}}

Liable for jet bridge conditions, boarding equipment, baggage handling, gate areas, and passenger assistance failures at their gates.

Ground Handling Companies

Menzies Aviation, Swissport, dnata handle baggage and ramp operations at {{airport_name}} — they carry their own liability policies.

Food & Retail Operators

HMSHost, SSP America, Paradies Lagardere, and Hudson Group operate most food and retail at {{airport_name}} and are liable for their leased areas.

TSA / Federal Government

Checkpoint injuries involve the federal government. Claims must be filed under the Federal Tort Claims Act before any lawsuit can proceed.
